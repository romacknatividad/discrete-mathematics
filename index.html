Why This Course Matters

Computer science depends on rigorous thinking. Discrete mathematics gives you the vocabulary and methods to analyze programs, prove correctness, reason about complexity, model networks, and understand recursive structure.

The emphasis here is not symbolic manipulation for its own sake. The emphasis is on constructing arguments, spotting structure, and solving computational problems.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the semester, students should be able to:

  • Write direct proofs, proofs by contradiction, and induction proofs.
  • Model computational problems using sets, relations, functions, and graphs.
  • Analyze counting problems with permutations, combinations, and recurrence relations.
  • Reason about discrete structures used in algorithms and software systems.

Semester Arc

Weekly Schedule

01

Logic and Mathematical Language

Propositions, predicates, quantifiers, logical equivalence, translating statements.

02

Proof Strategies

Direct proof, contrapositive, contradiction, cases, existence and uniqueness.

03

Sets, Functions, and Relations

Set operations, inverse images, injective and surjective functions, equivalence relations.

04

Algorithms and Number Theory

Divisibility, modular arithmetic, Euclidean algorithm, applications to computation.

05

Induction and Recursive Definitions

Weak induction, strong induction, recursive specifications, correctness arguments.

06

Counting Principles

Product and sum rules, permutations, combinations, binomial coefficients.

07

Advanced Counting

Pigeonhole principle, inclusion-exclusion, discrete probability foundations.

08

Midterm Review and Exam

Structured review, exam, and post-exam reflection on proof quality.

09

Recurrence Relations

Linear recurrences, closed forms, recursion trees, links to algorithm analysis.

10

Graph Theory I

Graphs, paths, cycles, connectivity, representations, graph modeling.

11

Graph Theory II

Trees, spanning trees, traversal, planar graphs, applications in networks.

12

Boolean Algebra and Logic Circuits

Boolean identities, switching circuits, simplification, digital logic connections.

13

Automata and Formal Reasoning

Finite-state models, language intuition, abstraction and state-based computation.

14

Synthesis and Project Presentations

Student presentations, connections across topics, final review.

Typical Weekly Workflow

  1. Read the assigned notes before lecture.
  2. Participate actively in worked examples and proof discussions.
  3. Complete the weekly problem set with written justification.
  4. Revise solutions after feedback to improve mathematical communication.

Project Ideas

  • Analyze the correctness of a simple graph algorithm.
  • Build a visual explainer for recurrence growth.
  • Study modular arithmetic in cryptographic protocols.
  • Compare proof patterns across several algorithmic claims.
